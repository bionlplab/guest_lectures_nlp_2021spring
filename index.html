Tentative Schedule

03/03/2021. Learning better representations from Electronic Health Records using Artificial Intelligence. Yanshan Wang.

The widespread adoption of Electronic Health Records (EHRs) has enabled the use of clinical data for clinical research and practice. Many institutions have established clinical data warehouses in conjunction with cohort discovery tools (e.g., i2b2) to support the use of clinical data for clinical research. Since a significant portion of relevant patient information is embedded in clinical narratives, natural language processing (NLP) techniques such as information extraction are critical when using EHR data for clinical research. However, the existing NLP methods are either optimized for a single information extraction task or have imperfect generalized performance. This problem is magnified when the extracted NLP artifacts are utilized in downstream applications, such as matching inclusion and exclusion criteria in cohort discovery. In this talk, I will present the Artificial Intelligence (AI) methods on EHRs using deep representation techniques to compensate for NLP approaches. I will discuss why learning better representations from EHRs is crucial for clinical applications of AI, and conclude with some potential future works.

03/10/2021. Recommendation Systems to Improve Reusability of Biomedical Datasets. Braja Gopal Patra.

Recent scientific discoveries have generated an extensive amount of data and these datasets are being stored in different repositories. Most of these datasets are used only once. Several biomedical dataset repositories are available integrated with search engines, which can help researchers looking to find specific types of datasets. DataMed (https://datamed.org) or Google Dataset Search may be helpful to researchers in finding relevant datasets. Researchers who generally want to find datasets related to their interests, but do not have a particular interest in mind, could benefit from a dataset recommendation system. Further, it is diﬀicult for researchers to keep track and search for new datasets related to their research field in the complex search environment. A dataset recommendation system will help solve the above searching problem and reduce the effort for searching suitable datasets. Once a dataset is found, a researcher may need to find relevant literature in the dataset’s domain. I will describe different content-based recommendation systems that we developed for finding datasets suitable for researchers and literature in the domain of datasets.

03/17/2021. Applying Machine Learning to Biomedical Literature Mining. Kyubum Lee.

Biomedical literature is a rich resource for obtaining key biomedical knowledge. Most of the new biomedical research findings are presented and published in peer-reviewed journals or conference publications, and indexed in literature databases such as PubMed or PubMed Central. However, considering that more than 3000 biomedical publications are indexed in PubMed every day, on average, finding relevant information is becoming increasingly difficult. In this talk, I will briefly introduce the available biomedical literature resources, and show a few case studies in which machine learning and more specifically text classification is used for extracting valuable information from biomedical literature.

03/24/2021. The application of Natural Language Processing to social media for public health. Jingcheng Du.

Social media provides the opportunity as well as the convenience for millions of users to generate, share, receive, and comment on social content at any time. The burgeoning popularity and its deep penetration to everyday life make social media a great resource to study health-related topics. As a significant portion of the data shared on social media is in the natural language format, Natural Language Processing (NLP) plays a major role in social media data mining. In this lecture, I will introduce several case studies at our research lab and beyond related to using NLP tools for social media data mining on public health topics.

03/31/2021. TBD. Lana Yeganova.

PubMed is a freely accessible system for searching biomedical literature and is one of the most widely used resources at the National Library of Medicine, NIH. PubMed is visited daily by millions of users, including scientific researchers, healthcare professionals, and general population. Continuous enhancement of PubMed search functionality is vital to better meet our users’ needs. Many components contribute to a successful search, including semantic understanding of queries and user intent, smart document indexing approaches, as well as state of the art search and ranking algorithms. In this talk, I will provide an overview of methods we have recently developed and incorporated into PubMed to improve literature search and user experience.

About the speakers

Yanshan Wang, Ph.D., is currently an Assistant Professor and Associate Consultant in the Department of Artificial Intelligence and Informatics at Mayo Clinic. His research is centered on developing novel natural language processing (NLP), artificial intelligence (AI), and machine learning methodologies for clinical applications. His research goal is to develop EHR data-driven AI solutions to accelerate clinical research and to facilitate clinical practice. He joined Mayo Clinic in 2015 as a Research Fellow, and became Research Associate and Associate Consultant in 2018 and 2019, respectively. Since Dr. Wang joined Mayo, he has served as Co-Investigators for multiple extramural NIH-funded projects and intramural operational projects. He has published over 60 peer-reviewed articles in high-impact medical informatics journals (e.g., JBI, JAMIA), and conferences (e.g., AMIA Annual Symposium, AMIA summit, IEEE BIBM). Dr. Wang is also active in organizing conference workshops and shared tasks in the medical informatics community, including the international Health NLP workshops and the renowned national NLP clinical challenge (n2c2).

Braja Gopal Patra, Ph.D., is a research associate in the Department of Population Health Sciences, WCM, Cornell University, New York, NY, USA. Previously, he was a postdoctoral fellow at the School of Public Health, University of Texas Health Science Center at Houston. He completed his Ph.D. in Computer Science and Engineering at Jadavpur University, Kolkata, India. His research interests include Natural Language Processing, Recommendation Systems, and Information Retrieval. His current research focuses on identifying social determinants of health from electronic health records using Natural Language Processing techniques.

Kyubum Lee, Ph.D., is a biomedical data scientist, and currently working as a postdoctoral fellow at Moffitt Cancer Center (Tampa, Florida). He received his Ph.D. in Computer Science from Korea University, specifically in machine learning and data mining. Before joining Moffitt Cancer Center, he worked as a postdoctoral fellow at the BioNLP lab of Dr. Zhiyong Lu in the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) at the National Institutes of Health (NIH). His expertise lies in machine learning and data mining. Most of his former projects involved using machine learning (including deep learning) for extracting valuable information from big biomedical text data. His current research focuses on applying machine learning and data mining techniques to cancer genomic research tasks specifically genomic data analysis and histopathology image data analysis.

Jingcheng Du, Ph.D., is an assistant professor in health informatics at UTHealth School of Biomedical Informatics (SBMI). He received a Ph.D. in biomedical informatics from UTHealth SBMI. Dr. Du is an Associate Editor of BMC Medical Informatics and Decision Making, and serve as ad-hoc reviewers and program committee members for many peer-reviewed journals and conferences respectively. Dr. Du’s research focuses on machine learning and natural language processing and their applications on clinical and social media data. He is currently serving as co-investigators for two NIH grants and one AHA grant.
